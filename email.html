Email Phishing

What is "Phishing"?


"The practice of sending fraudulent emails pretending to be from well-known and highly used companies in order to entice individuals to reveal personal information, mainly financial data." Disguises a cyber-attack as an email aiming to trick the recipient into believing the content of the email is true. It is used to steal confidential information from the recipient or an organisation.

Who it affects

Organisations are the usual victims - to attack the organisation, attackers will target workers within the organisation. However, anyone with an email address can be a victim, although it's more likely to be the older generation (who have more money and are more susceptible to being tricked by this method).

In 2017, 6.2 billion attempts were made to attack businesses/organisations on the internet.
Phishing was responsible for 73% of the attacks.

Most Common Phishing Scenario

Receive a fake Email

You get an email in your inbox from your bank asking you to verify some account information.

Click the link

You click the link in the email and it takes you to a website which looks nearly identically to your banks website.

Enter Private Information

Once you have entered your information to the fake website, you will be redirected to the real website to enter your information a second time.

Phishing can also be used to download malicious software (malware) onto your device If you click an attachment or even a link which is unsafe, it could download one of millions of malicious software onto your device which can cause harm such as stealing all your information/data to hijacking your device.

When it can happen

Phishing is a social engineering attack - tricking someone into exposing personal information - used to steal user data meaning it can happen at any point. There isn't a particular time the phishing can or cannot happen.

Where it can happen

Through email or even text message. Could be personal email or work email. With Covid-19 causing remote working, workers are targeted a lot more. Countries with high cybercrime rates as well as advanced technology are the most common places for phishing

Why it happens

To steal your information or your identity or to steal funds from your bank account. Could also be to attack your contacts or to gain access to your organisations.

How to avoid it


- Learn how to spot fake emails.

- Familiarise yourself with emails from big brands you use frequently.

- Do more research into phishing emails to familiarise yourself with different types.

- Do not click attachments and links in emails you are suspicious of - could contain harmful software.

- Use the rollover feature - hover your mouse over the link and see where the link really takes you.

- Double check every URL of the websites you are visiting. If it is an organisations website which is highly reputable, it will begin with "https" - with the "s" meaning secure. This means you're safe. As well as the beginning, the spelling of the web address needs to be checked to make sure you're not going to a completely different site.

- Relying on technology also is a good way to avoid it. Emails services automatically block emails they class as "spam" which work the majority of the time, but never fully rely on technology.


Google recently implemented a system into their browser notifying users if they may be on a fraudulent website due to an incorrect spelling in the URL.
